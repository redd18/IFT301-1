13.10 What are the advantages of using CDMA for a cellular network?

Frequency diversity: with frequency diversity, the signal is spread out over a larger frequency bandwidth, frequency-dependent transmission impairments, such as noise bursts and selective fading, have less effect on the signal.

Multipath resistance: The chipping codes used for CDMA not only exhibit low cross-correlation but also low auto correlation. Therefore, a version of the signal that is delayed by more than one chip interval does not interfere with the dominant signal as much as in other multipath environments.

Privacy: Because spread spectrum is obtained by the use of noise-like signals, where each user has a unique code, privacy is inherent.

Graceful degradation: With FDMA or TDMA, a fixed number of users can simultaneously access the system. However, with CDMA, as more users simultaneously access the system, the noise level and hence the error rate increases; only gradually does the system degrade to the point of an unacceptable error rate

13.11 What are the disadvantages of using CDMA for a cellular network?

Self-jamming: Unless all of the mobile users are perfectly synchronized, the arriving transmissions from multiple users will not be perfectly aligned on chip boundaries. Thus the spreading sequences of the different users are not orthogonal and there is some level of cross-correlation. This is distinct from either TDMA or FDMA, in which for reasonable time or frequency guard bands, respectively, the received signals are orthogonal or nearly so.

Near-far problem: Signals closer to the receiver are received with less attenuation than signals farther away. Given the lack of complete orthogonality, the transmissions from the more remote mobile units may be more difficult to recover. Thus, power control techniques are very important in a CDMA system.

Soft handoff: A smooth handoff from one cell to the next requires that the mobile acquires the new cell before it relinquishes the old. This is referred to as a soft handoff, and is more complex than the hard handoff used in FDMA and TDMA schemes.

    5. Explain the difference between a hard hand-off and a soft hand-off.

    In hard handoff the mobile station switches to a new frequency band that is within the allocation of the new cell while in soft handoff the mobile station is temporarily connected to more than one base station simultaneously.

    8. Explain the purpose of a SIM card.

    SIM cards are used for user identification associated with a mobile station. They also provide the ability to distinguish between equipment mobility and subscriber mobility.

    9. Briefly describe the registration process for a GSM cellular phone.

    MS sends a request to the BSS. The BSS gathers information about the MS and forwards it to the MSC/VLR. The VLR checks to see if the MS is registered in the database. If not then the VLR gets a copy of the subscription information from the HLR. HLR validates the MS’s IMSI. If valid then the VLR stores it in its database and sends an acknowledgement to the MS.

    10. What is the difference between a control channel and a traffic channel in a cellular radio system?

    Control channels are used to exchange information having to do with setting up and maintaining calls and with establishing a relationship between a mobile unit and the nearest BS. Traffic channels carry a voice or data connection between users.

    11. What are the major IMEI categories? Identify the purpose of each.

    White List – register of all equipment
    Black List – register of all suspended equipment
    Gray List – register of malfunctioning or obsolete equipment

    12. How are the MSISDN and the MSRN different? How are they similar?

    MSISDN is the actual telephone number of a mobile unit and the MSRN has the same structure as a MSISDN but is a temporary location-dependent ISDN number.

    13. How does a cellular phone identify its current location within an area?

    A cellular phone uses the HLR to identify its current location with an area. The HLR contains a link to the current location of the MS.

    14. How are the IMSI and the TMSI different? How are they similar?

    The IMSI is stored in the SIM and used to uniquely identify a subscriber and the TMSI is used in place of the IMSI for the definite identification and addressing of the MS.

    15. What is the purpose of the BSIC?

    The BSIC is used to distinguish neighboring base stations.

    16. How are the VLR and HLR different? How are the two similar?

    The HLR has data for every subscriber and every mobile ISDN number in the respective network and the VLR stores the data of all MSs which are currently staying in the administrative area of the associated MSC.
